AB  - Summary Melanocytic tumors of the oral cavity, melanoma and melanocytoma, are frequently diagnosed in the dog and are diagnosed far less often in other species. Melanomas invade soft tissues and bone and may be associated with extensive local tissue destruction. Although fibrous tissue-based lesions can share substantial histological overlap, these lesions can vary in their biological behavior and long-term prognosis. The canine biologically high-grade/histologically low-grade fibrosarcoma (H/L FSA) is an interesting and problematic mesenchymal lesion, first reported in 1994 by Ciekot et al. The chapter discusses the neoplastic entities fibroma and FSA. Some equine oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC) lesions are so poorly differentiated, it is difficult to determine if they have an epithelial or mesenchymal histogenesis. As for dogs, investigations have rarely identified papillomavirus DNA sequences in feline OSCC lesions. Although lymphoma occurs in many different veterinary species, oral lymphoma most often occurs in dogs and cats and less often in horses, cattle, and goats.
