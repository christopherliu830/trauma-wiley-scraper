AB  - Summary There are different strategies available for feeding infants in the first 4-6 months of life, and these include breastfeeding, bottle-feeding a formula and a mixed approach utilizing both breast and formula milks. This chapter reviews the processes through which human milk is produced and why breastfeeding is optimal for health and development. Human milk production is under endocrine control through actions of prolactin and oxytocin. Breastfeeding delivers immunoprotective factors to the infant, in addition to nutrients in an optimal formulation. It is associated with lower risk of sudden infant death syndrome, childhood obesity and allergies in susceptible infants. The decision to use alternative feeding methods is largely shaped by social and cultural factors. Formulas designed for bottle-fed babies are generally manufactured using modified by-products of the dairy industry. Formula milks based upon sheep or goat's milk may be promoted as an alternative to cow's milk formula.

AB  - Summary Biomaterials interact with the living system. Depending on the location and the nature of the biomaterial, the response of the body to the biomaterial may vary. Biomaterial implantation within the host causes an injury. There are two broad phenomenon taking place after every biomaterial implant: tissue response and biomaterial response. The beginning of tissue response is marked by the interaction of blood proteins with the biomaterial. Unlike living tissue, implanted biomaterials are not ?rejected,? but they still elicit an immune response called foreign body response. Biomaterials at times may give out leachates, which cause allergy in the host system and/or other immune responses. The dynamic interplay between the host cells/tissue and the biomaterial, shall decide the level of biocompatibility. Stability and sustained functionality focus on the integrity of the material and the host tissue throughout the implant lifetime; these terms bring in the facet of material quality into the picture.
