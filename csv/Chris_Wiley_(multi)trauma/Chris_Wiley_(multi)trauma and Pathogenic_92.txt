AB  - Abstract Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) is the often fatal sequelae of a broad range of precipitating conditions. Despite decades of intensive research and clinical trials there remain no therapies in routine clinical practice that target the dysregulated and overwhelming inflammatory response that characterises ARDS. Neutrophils play a central role in the initiation, propagation and resolution of this complex inflammatory environment by migrating into the lung and executing a variety of pro-inflammatory functions. These include degranulation with liberation of bactericidal proteins, release of cytokines and reactive oxygen species as well as production of neutrophil extracellular traps. Although these functions are advantageous in clearing bacterial infection, the consequence of associated tissue damage, the contribution to worsening acute inflammation and prolonged neutrophil lifespan at sites of inflammation are deleterious. In this review, the importance of the neutrophil will be considered, together with discussion of recent advances in understanding neutrophil function and the factors that influence them throughout the phases of inflammation in ARDS. From a better understanding of neutrophils in this context, potential therapeutic targets are identified and discussed. ? 2018 The Authors. AB  - Summary While ticks, and probably tick-borne infections, have been present throughout human history, their level of impact on human health has been growing in recent years as new tick-bone diseases (TBDs), new ranges for such diseases, and tick-borne disease-interactions have been identified. These changes are the consequence of several socioenvironmental factors, including loss of bio-diversity, changing biopolitics, and varied human behavioral and settlement patterns across time and location. In the closing decades of the 20th century and opening decades of the 21st century, TBD epidemics have broken out in several countries, some with high case-mortality rates or long-term morbidity and associated social suffering. Despite the global health consequences of TBDs, there remain significant gaps in our understanding of these threats to human health. One such gap involves understanding the adverse health effects of suffering multiple interacting TBDs, or what are here called tick-borne disease syndemics. This chapter explores the behaviors during the Anthropocene that have increased syndemic interaction of TBDs and the growing contribution of these occurrences to a global tick-borne disease burden.
