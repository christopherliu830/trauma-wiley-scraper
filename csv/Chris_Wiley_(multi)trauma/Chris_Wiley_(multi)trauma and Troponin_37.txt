AB  - Summary Acute limb ischemia (ALI) is a limb- and life-threatening condition that necessitates prompt recognition and treatment. This chapter reviews the epidemiology, etiology, clinical presentation, and treatment of ALI. Diagnostic and therapeutic treatment strategies are presented in the context of the best available evidence in the field. The primary causes for ALI may be categorized into two major groups: embolic and thrombotic. Catheter-directed thrombolysis (CDT) may be less desirable in a patient with underlying anemia or risk of bleeding, since systemic effects of thrombolytic agents may worsen these issues. The initial treatment of a patient with a cold limb and high suspicion for ALI should include the institution of intravenous unfractionated heparin. The administration of aspirin, while generally recommended for primary or secondary prevention purposes, can be considered in most patients presenting with ALI, assuming no drug allergy or concurrent bleeding diathesis.

AB  - Summary This chapter focuses on the conditions more commonly encountered, either in medical examinations or in clinical practice, and attempts to provide a framework for developing an understanding of their pathological basis and diagnosis. Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) can be used in the diagnosis of diabetes mellitus (DM) and to distinguish between the other causes of hyperglycaemia. Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) is a life-threatening acute complication of DM, occurring when insulin therapy is absent or becomes inadequate for the current physiological state. Diabetes insipidus (DI) is a condition associated with either abnormal antidiuretic hormone (ADH) secretion from the posterior pituitary (central) or an abnormal renal response to ADH. Cortisol is a corticosteroid hormone produced by the adrenal cortex. Cushing's syndrome is caused by an excess of glucocorticoids. Multiple endocrine neoplasia is a rare syndrome involving the development of either multiple endocrine gland tumours or hyperplasia.
